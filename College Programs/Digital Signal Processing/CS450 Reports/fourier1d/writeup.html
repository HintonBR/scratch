Part A - Sin
Sin is an odd function so its imaginary part is nonzero while it's real part is 0 (any values are simply due to imprecision in floating point arithmetic). The magnitude flips signs on the ends due to the squaring of the imaginary number to drag the -1 out from under the square root.

Part A - Cosine
Cosine is an even function so its imaginary part is 0 and its real part is positive on both ends. The image of the imaginary part shows data, but the scale of it is so close to 0 that the points represent small floating point errors in the data and not real values in the imaginary portion of the signal.

Part A - Cosine + Sin
The sum is simply the sum of the parts except for the magnitude which is the square root of the sum of the squares of the imaginary and real parts of the signal. This is shown in the fact that the real part of the signal looks like the real part of the cosine because the real part of the sine is 0 and the imaginary is just the opposite, it shows the imaginary part of the sin because the imag. part of a cosine is 0. The phases of the two signals add to simply the phase portion of the signal because of varying signs at different points.
